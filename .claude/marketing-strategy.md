# TalentWharf — Marketing Strategy & Launch Playbook

*Created: 2026-02-22*
*Budget: $0 — founder-led organic*
*Primary channel: LinkedIn*
*Launch type: Invite-only beta*
*Goal: 20-50 active startup teams*

---

## Part 1: The Narrative (What We're Selling)

We're not selling a tool. We're selling a belief about hiring.

**The belief:** Culture fit starts with the first message. The best hires aren't sourced from job boards — they self-select. They DM you on LinkedIn. They email your founder. They tell a friend "I love what that company is building." But startups lose track of those people. When it's time to hire, they start from zero — posting jobs, sorting strangers, paying recruiters — when the right people already raised their hand months ago.

**The product:** TalentWharf helps you organize cold messages and remember great people. So when a role opens, your best candidates are already waiting.

**The narrative arc (for all content):**
1. People who love your company reach out
2. Those messages get buried and forgotten
3. You end up hiring strangers instead of believers
4. TalentWharf captures that inbound interest
5. When you're ready to hire, you start with people who chose you first

## Part 2: Launch Strategy (Invite-Only Beta)

### Phase 1: Pre-Launch Warm-Up (Weeks 1-2)

**Goal:** Build anticipation and a waitlist of 100-200 interested people before opening beta.

**Actions:**

1. **LinkedIn "belief post" series** (3 posts, 1 per week)
   - Post 1: The origin story — "I realized the best hires we ever made all started with a cold message..."
   - Post 2: The problem — "I searched my LinkedIn DMs for someone who messaged us 6 months ago. Couldn't find them. We'd already hired someone else who quit in 3 months."
   - Post 3: The announcement — "I built something to fix this. Looking for 50 startup teams to try it."

2. **Direct outreach to your network** (manual, 10-15 per day)
   - Message founders/recruiters you know personally
   - Not a pitch — a conversation: "I'm working on something for startup hiring, curious if you've ever lost track of someone who reached out wanting to work with you?"
   - If they relate → invite to beta

3. **Build a simple waitlist page**
   - One page. One headline. One email field.
   - Headline: "Never lose track of people who want to work with you."
   - Subhead: "TalentWharf helps startups organize cold messages and remember great people. Free invite-only beta."
   - CTA: "Request early access"

### Phase 2: Beta Launch (Weeks 3-6)

**Goal:** Get first 20-30 teams onboarded and actively using the product.

**Actions:**

1. **Invite in batches** (not all at once)
   - Batch 1: 10 teams (people you know, most likely to give honest feedback)
   - Batch 2: 10 teams (warm contacts from waitlist)
   - Batch 3: 10-30 teams (everyone else on the waitlist)
   - Why batches: you can fix onboarding friction between each wave

2. **Onboarding concierge** (manual, high-touch)
   - Personally onboard every beta team in the first two batches
   - 15-minute call or async walkthrough
   - Ask them to capture their first 5 candidates during onboarding
   - Follow up after 3 days: "Have you found anyone you'd forgotten about?"

3. **Collect stories, not testimonials**
   - Don't ask "would you recommend us?"
   - Ask: "Have you found anyone in your DMs or email that you'd forgotten about?"
   - Those stories become your content engine

4. **Continue LinkedIn content** (2x per week)
   - Share real observations from beta (anonymized)
   - "One of our beta teams found 8 people in their LinkedIn DMs who'd messaged about working there. 3 of them were perfect for an open role."
   - The narrative writes itself once people start using it

### Phase 3: Growth & Word of Mouth (Weeks 7-12)

**Goal:** Hit 50 teams, establish word-of-mouth loop, create content flywheel.

**Actions:**

1. **Referral mechanic** (simple, no code needed)
   - "Know a startup team that should be using this? Forward them this invite link."
   - Each beta team gets 3 invite codes to share
   - People who get invited skip the waitlist

2. **Community seeding**
   - Identify 5-10 Slack/Discord communities where startup founders hang out
   - Don't spam. Participate genuinely for 2 weeks first.
   - When hiring questions come up, share the belief (not the tool): "We found that the best hires are people who already reached out to us"
   - If someone asks "how do you track that?" — natural product mention

3. **Content compounding**
   - Turn beta stories into LinkedIn posts
   - Turn LinkedIn posts into a short blog series
   - Turn the blog series into a "How Startups Should Think About Hiring" guide

## Part 3: LinkedIn Content Strategy (Your #1 Channel)

### Content Pillars

1. **The Belief** (40% of posts)
   - Culture fit, first messages, self-selecting candidates
   - Hot takes on startup hiring that come from conviction
   - "The best hire I ever made started with a LinkedIn DM"

2. **The Problem** (30% of posts)
   - Stories about lost candidates, buried DMs, hiring regrets
   - Relatable frustrations startup founders share
   - "I just searched my LinkedIn messages for 'want to work' — found 12 conversations I never followed up on"

3. **The Proof** (20% of posts)
   - Beta insights, anonymized stories, product moments
   - "One team found..." or "In the first week of beta..."
   - Screenshots of the product in action (dashboard, AI matches)

4. **The Invite** (10% of posts)
   - Direct beta invitations, waitlist openings
   - Only after you've earned attention with the other 3 pillars
   - "We just opened 10 more beta spots. Link in comments."

### Posting Cadence
- **Weeks 1-2:** 3 posts/week (building narrative)
- **Weeks 3-6:** 2 posts/week (beta stories + belief posts)
- **Weeks 7+:** 2-3 posts/week (mix of all pillars)

### Post Format Guidelines
- **Hook in first line.** LinkedIn truncates after ~2 lines. Your first sentence must stop the scroll.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-2 sentences max. Walls of text die on LinkedIn.
- **End with a question or CTA.** Comments boost reach. Ask something people want to answer.
- **No links in post body.** LinkedIn suppresses posts with external links. Put links in comments.
- **Personal > promotional.** Write as a founder sharing a belief, not a company pushing a product.

### Example Posts (Ready to Adapt)

**Post 1 — The Origin Story:**
```
The best engineer we ever hired sent me a cold DM 8 months before we had a role open.

She said: "I've been following your product for a while. If you ever need someone on the backend team, I'd love to chat."

I liked the message. Thought "cool, I'll remember her."

I didn't remember her.

6 months later, we opened a backend role. Posted on job boards. Sorted through 150 applications. Hired someone mid. They left in 4 months.

Then one day I was scrolling old DMs and found her message.

She'd already taken another job.

That's when I realized: the best candidates don't come from job boards. They come from people who already chose you. But we have no system for remembering them.

So I built one.

Has this ever happened to you?
```

**Post 2 — The Belief:**
```
Hot take: Culture fit isn't something you screen for in an interview.

It's established the moment someone decides to send that message.

"Hey, I love what you're building. Are you hiring?"

That message tells you more about culture fit than any behavioral interview question ever will.

It says: I found you. I chose you. I want to be part of this.

But most startups have no system for capturing that signal. Those DMs get buried. Those emails get archived. Those intros get forgotten.

Then when you're hiring, you post on a job board and sort through people who applied to 50 companies this week.

What if you started every hire with the people who already raised their hand?
```

**Post 3 — The Problem:**
```
I just did an experiment.

Searched my LinkedIn DMs for the phrase "would love to chat."

Found 23 conversations from the last year.

People who reached out wanting to work with us. Engineers, designers, PMs.

I replied to exactly 0 of them at the time.

Not because they weren't good. Because I wasn't hiring when they messaged. And by the time I was hiring, I'd completely forgotten they existed.

23 people who self-selected. Gone.

How many are sitting in your inbox right now?
```

## Part 5: Cold-Start Growth Playbook

### The Core Loop
```
Belief content on LinkedIn
    → Attracts startup founders who relate
        → They join the waitlist
            → They get invited to beta
                → They find forgotten candidates in their DMs
                    → That "aha" becomes a story
                        → The story becomes your next LinkedIn post
                            → Loop repeats
```

### Channels (Ranked by Priority)

| Priority | Channel | Why | Effort |
|----------|---------|-----|--------|
| 1 | LinkedIn (organic) | Your audience lives here. Hiring discussions happen here. Free. | 3-4 hrs/week |
| 2 | Direct DMs | Highest conversion. Personal asks to founders you know. | 1-2 hrs/week |
| 3 | Startup Slack/Discord communities | Founders helping founders. Natural product mention opportunities. | 1 hr/week |
| 4 | Indie Hackers / Hacker News | Builders appreciate builder stories. Good for launch moment. | Event-based |
| 5 | Product Hunt | Save for after beta when you have stories + stable product. | One-time prep |
| 6 | Twitter/X | Secondary to LinkedIn for your audience, but good for amplification. | Optional |

### Metrics to Track (Weekly)

| Metric | Week 1-2 Target | Week 3-6 Target | Week 7-12 Target |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Waitlist signups | 30-50 | 100+ | 200+ |
| Beta teams onboarded | 0 | 20-30 | 50 |
| LinkedIn post impressions | 1K+ | 3K+ | 5K+ |
| Candidates captured (all teams) | 0 | 100+ | 500+ |
| Referral invites sent | 0 | 10+ | 30+ |
| "Aha" stories collected | 0 | 3-5 | 10+ |

### What Success Looks Like at 12 Weeks
- 50 startup teams actively using TalentWharf
- 500+ candidates captured across all teams
- 5-10 genuine "I found someone I'd forgotten about" stories
- A LinkedIn presence that startup founders recognize
- Enough signal to decide: PMF or pivot

## Appendix: Things NOT to Do

- **Don't lead with features.** "AI matching" and "Chrome extension" are how, not why. Lead with the belief.
- **Don't compare to competitors.** You're not a "better Greenhouse." You're a different category.
- **Don't do paid ads yet.** $0 budget means every dollar of attention must be earned. That's fine — belief-driven content earns more attention than ads anyway.
- **Don't launch on Product Hunt before beta.** You need stories and a stable product first. PH is a one-shot moment.
- **Don't spray and pray in communities.** Earn trust first. Help people. The product mentions come naturally.
- **Don't write corporate copy.** Write like a founder talking to another founder. Personal > polished.
